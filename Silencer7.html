2. THEORY

2.1. General Considerations

The theory used throughout this thesis is that developed by Scott in [ref. 3] in which it is assumed that the duct is rectangular. Scott further assumes that by symmetry the component of the particle velocity normal to the central plane of the duct is zero. This boundary condition limits the analysis to those modes that have a plane of pressure amplitude maxima along the duct axis. Wassilieff [ref. 9; § 2] has rightly interpreted this but has unfortunately written that there "...is zero particle velocity at the centre line of the duct axis" and that only the symmetric modes (0,0), (0,2) and (0,4) etc. can give solutions to Scott's equations. This is in fact incorrect for the (0,0) mode which cannot always give zero particle velocity along the centre line. What should be written is that the component of the particle velocity normal to the central plane parallel to the absorbing sides is zero.

'Kinsler & Frey' [ref. 11; § 9.8; Fig. 9.8] and Morse and Ingard [ref. 10; § 9.2; pp.493-494] show schematically that for the (0,1) mode there is a nodal plane for pressure amplitude midway between the walls of a rectangular, hard walled, duct; this is a plane of maximum normal particle velocity amplitude, a condition at odds with that required in Scott's paper. The possible modes of propagation in a rectangular duct are (m,n) where m or n is 0 or any positive integer, see again [refs. 10 & 11]. The (m,0) mode is absorbed by the wall linings similarly for all values of m in Scott's analysis since the absorbing material is placed symmetrically about the central plane, which is co-planar with \(y=0\), see Fig. 2.1. The analysis is as valid for an air space between two parallel absorbing planes of infinite extent as for a rectangular duct with two parallel walls absorbing and the others totally reflecting. The modes of propagation that can therefore be distinguished as having different propagation constants are (0,n) where n = 0, 2, 4, etc. as in [ref. 9].

The properties of the porous material are regarded as being solely described by its flow resistivity and the frequency as shown by Delany & Bazley [ref. 6] and as modified by Mechel, below, see [ref. 12], see also Vér [ref. 13; eqs. 14 & 15] and Munjal [ref. 14; eqs. 6.29 & 6.30].

For \(A \le 60\); where \(A = \sigma / (\rho \cdot f)\)

\[\Gamma_a / k = 0.189 \cdot A^{0.6185} + j \cdot \left[1 + 0.0978 \cdot A^{-0.6929}\right]\] \[Z_a / Z_0 = 1 + 0.04885 \cdot A^{0.754} - j \cdot 0.087 \cdot A^{0.7307} \quad\quad (2.1.1)\]

For \(A > 60\):

\[\Gamma_a / k = \left[-1.4664 + j \cdot 0.2115 \cdot A\right]^{\frac{1}{2}}\] \[Z_a / Z_0 = \frac{(A / 2\pi) + j \cdot 1.4035}{\Gamma_a / k} \quad\quad (2.1.2)\]

Please note that there is a printing error in Mechel's original article and mistakes of a more gross nature have unfortunately found their way into [refs. 13 & 14] as referred to above. The equations which are also to be found correctly printed in [ref. 12] are as written above. These form the basis of the subroutine 'Material' used together with 'Extended' in the four-pole programme.

Scott has also derived an equation for the propagation constant for ducts with circular cross sections lined with a layer of uniform thickness of homogeneous, isotropic porous material, see [ref. 3; § 4]. This is again transcendental and contains a number of Bessel and Neumann functions of zeroth and first order, the solution of which is not attempted in this thesis. Mechel in [ref. 15] describes an iterative procedure for "absorbers with sound propagation parallel to the duct axis in the interior of the absorber material,..." and gives in an appendix the numerical computation of Bessel functions.

This thesis deals with ducts of circular or non-rectangular cross-sections and rectangular ducts with absorbing material on all sides, in the manner described by Mechel in [ref. 16] and which was arrived at independently by the author using the following argument.

From Munjal [ref. 14; § 6.6; p. 244] the acoustic attenuation is known to be proportional to the ratio of lined perimeter to flow area. The attenuation is directly proportional to the real part of the propagation constant \((Re\{\Gamma\})\). Let \(R\) be the radius and \(H\) and \(W\) the height and width of circular and rectangular ducts respectively, then the real part of the propagation constant can be written as follows:

\[Re\{\Gamma(p)\} \propto \frac{2 \cdot H}{H \cdot W} \quad \text{for rectangular ducts with porous material on two parallel sides}\] \[Re\{\Gamma(s)\} \propto \frac{2 \cdot (H + W)}{H \cdot W} \quad \text{for rectangular ducts with porous material all around the inside}\] \[Re\{\Gamma(c)\} \propto \frac{2 \cdot \pi \cdot R}{\pi \cdot R \cdot R} \quad \text{for circular ducts with porous material all around the inside}\]

If the rectangular duct is square then \(W=H\) and if \(2 \cdot R = H\) then one has:

\[Re\{\Gamma(p)\} = 2 \cdot Re\{\Gamma(s)\} = 2 \cdot Re\{\Gamma(c)\} \quad\quad (2.1.3)\]

This factor of 2 is accounted for in the subroutine and algorithm 'Extended' in programme lines 10960 to 11060. The approach adopted above is assumed to be valid for plane wave propagation but not for higher order modes where the actual duct geometry plays a more important role. It is further assumed that the lining thickness is constant and that there is no flow present, the Mach number is therefore zero. Viscous losses at the walls are ignored and the medium (air) is also assumed to be inviscid.

2.1.1. Some Comments on the Four-Pole Method

It is interesting to note that the four-pole matrix, see [ref. 7; § 4] is mathematically identical to the Mach or Lorentz transformation. This is actually not too surprising since the whole idea behind the Mach transformation is to leave the wave equation invariant. The wave maintains its shape and phase velocity leading to what is called the invariance of the interval. The wave has this behaviour also in the mathematically banal case of a lateral translation, which is after all what is happening in the four-pole example where the point of observation is translated from one end of the duct to the other. The Mach transformation can also be put to use in duct acoustics when flow is present.

2.2. Scott's Theory

The field equations for the air space and lining respectively are:

\[v = -\text{grad } \Phi, \quad p = j \omega \rho \Phi, \quad \nabla^2\Phi + k^2\Phi = 0\] \[v = -\text{grad } \Theta, \quad p = j \omega \rho' \Theta, \quad \nabla^2\Theta + h^2\Theta = 0 \quad\quad (2.2.1)\]

Scott suggested that for any one mode of propagation along the duct \(\Phi\) and \(\Theta\) will have a common propagation constant \(\Gamma\) such that for a rectangular duct, see Fig. 2.1:

\[\Phi = \Phi(y) \cdot e^{-\Gamma x} \cdot e^{j\omega t}\] \[\Theta = \Theta(y) \cdot e^{-\Gamma x} \cdot e^{j\omega t} \quad\quad (2.2.2)\]

Substituting these equations into the field equations above for the air space and lining respectively gives:

\[\Phi(y) = A \cdot e^{j\lambda y} + B \cdot e^{-j\lambda y}, \quad \lambda^2 = \Gamma^2 + k^2\] \[\Theta(y) = C \cdot e^{j\mu y} + D \cdot e^{-j\mu y}, \quad \mu^2 = \Gamma^2 + h^2 \quad\quad (2.2.3)\]

Where \(A, B, C\) and \(D\) are constants yet to be found. Scott argues that along the central plane \(y=0\) the gradient \(\partial\Phi/\partial y = 0\) by symmetry; this is the condition for zero normal particle velocity (see § 2.1). Therefore:

\[A = B\]

At the outer wall (assumed rigid) \(y=l+d\) and \(\partial\Theta/\partial y = 0\) i.e. particle velocity normal to the wall is zero therefore:

\[j\mu C \cdot e^{j\mu(l+d)} - j\mu D \cdot e^{-j\mu(l+d)} = 0\] \[\text{or} \quad D = C \cdot e^{2j\mu(l+d)} \quad\quad (2.2.4)\]

(N.B. in Scott's original paper \(d\) is the lining thickness and \(l\) is the width of the air space). At the lining/air surface, \(y=l\), there is both continuity of normal particle velocity and acoustic pressure giving:

\[j\lambda A \cdot e^{j\lambda l} - j\lambda B \cdot e^{-j\lambda l} = j\mu C \cdot e^{j\mu l} - j\mu D \cdot e^{-j\mu l}\] \[\text{and} \quad \rho \cdot \left[A \cdot e^{j\lambda l} + B \cdot e^{-j\lambda l}\right] = \rho' \cdot \left[C \cdot e^{j\mu l} + D \cdot e^{-j\mu l}\right] \quad\quad (2.2.5)\]

Substituting for \(B\) and \(D\) and eliminating \(A\) and \(C\) gives:

\[\rho \cdot \frac{\coth(-j\lambda l)}{\lambda} = \rho' \cdot \frac{\coth(j\mu d)}{\mu}\]

The above equation can be written in the following way:

\[\mu \rho \cdot \tan(\mu d) = -\lambda \rho' \cdot \tan(\lambda l)\]

which using eq. 2.2.3 can be written as:

\[\rho \cdot [\Gamma^2 + h^2]^{\frac{1}{2}} \tan([\Gamma^2 + h^2]^{\frac{1}{2}} d) = -\rho' \cdot [\Gamma^2 + k^2]^{\frac{1}{2}} \tan([\Gamma^2 + k^2]^{\frac{1}{2}} l) \quad\quad (2.2.6)\]

Christie [ref. 5] uses the following variables:

\[w = [(\Gamma l)^2 + (kl)^2]^{\frac{1}{2}} \quad\quad (2.2.7)\] \[p = [(hl)^2 - (kl)^2]^{\frac{1}{2}}, \quad b = d/l; \quad M = \rho/\rho' \quad\quad (2.2.8)\]

allowing eq. 2.2.6 to be written in the following manner:

\[f(w) = w \cdot \tan w + M[w^2 + p^2]^{\frac{1}{2}} \cdot \tan(b[w^2 + p^2]^{\frac{1}{2}}) = 0 \quad\quad (2.2.9)\]

This equation is as mentioned before transcendental and has therefore to be solved either using the Newton-Raphson method or some other iterative procedure such as Muller's method, see [ref. 17; § 2.5].

2.2.1. The properties of the lining

Christie [ref. 5] leaves the factor \(M\) as the ratio \(\rho/\rho'\) stating that eq. 2.2.9 is solved with the help of Delany and Bazley's empirical formulae, without showing how \(M\) has been expanded and with which assumptions. (Writing before the modifications of Mechel, these formulae must necessarily be the original version in [ref. 6] which may in part account for the slight discrepancies between Christie's calculations and those obtained by the author, see Fig. 1.1.)

Kurze and Vér [ref. 18; § 2] writing, about non-isotropic linings, a year later than Christie, argue that: "Compression losses are small and the bulk modulus \(K\) is assumed to be real. At low frequencies, the compression in fibrous materials is isothermal and \(K\) equals the ambient static pressure \(P\). Only at high frequencies does \(K\) assume the free-field value for adiabatic compression, i.e. \(K = 1.4P = \rho \cdot c^2\) where \(c\) is the free-field sound velocity". Wassilieff [ref. 9; § 2] takes exception to this, writing concerning [ref. 18], that: "There is a factor \(\rho \cdot c^2/K\) lacking, where \(K\) is the bulk modulus of the lining. This has probably occurred as a result of the common practice of assuming it to be equal to unity. The magnitude of \(\rho \cdot c^2/K\), in fact, varies from 1.4 at low frequencies to 1.0 at high frequencies. This approximation renders the predictions of Kurze and Vér's equation conservative, thus providing a possible explanation for their own observations regarding poor agreement with experiment". See also the comments on eq. 2.2.20 in § 2.2.2 on p. 19.

With the aid of eq. 2.2.1, the boundary condition of equal normal particle velocity at the lining/air interface which in [ref. 9; § 2] is written as

\[\partial\Phi/\partial y = \partial\Theta/\partial y\]

can be written as follows by rewriting the eqs

\[\Phi = (1/j\omega\rho)p(air) \quad \text{and} \quad \Theta = (1/j\omega\rho')p(lining)\]

and writing \(p(air)\) as \(p\) and \(p(lining)\) as \(p'\), thus giving

\[\partial p/\partial y = (\rho/\rho')\partial p'/\partial y \quad\quad (2.2.10)\]

Scott in [ref. 4; § 2; eq. 8] shows that the complex density \(\rho'\) can be written (using Scott's notation) as:

\[\rho' = h^2 K / (P \omega^2) \quad\quad (2.2.11)\]

where (kappa) \(K\) is called by Scott the effective compressibility of the air in the pore space and \(P\), in this case, is the porosity. Scott writes: "..by definition \(p = s \cdot \kappa\).." where \(s\) is the condensation, it can be seen therefore that \(\kappa\) is in fact the bulk modulus \(K\), see [ref. 11; § 5.2; eq. 5.5; p. 101]. (Convention today has that compressibility and bulk modulus are mutually reciprocal.)

The complex density is related to the characteristic impedance of the lining in the following way, (see Scott [ref. 4; § 3; eq. 13])

\[Z_a = \rho' \omega / h \quad\quad (2.2.12)\]

Which on substitution into eq. 2.2.10, and writing \(\omega = ck\), gives

\[\partial p/\partial y = (\rho c k / Z_a h)\partial p'/\partial y \quad\quad (2.2.13)\]

Also, substituting for \(\rho'\) in eq. 2.2.10 with eq. 2.2.11 (with \(c \cdot k = \omega\) and \(k = K\)) gives

\[\partial p/\partial y = (\rho c^2 / K) \cdot (k^2 P / h^2)\partial p'/\partial y \quad\quad (2.2.14)\]

Identifying the porosity \(P\), above, with \(\sigma\) in [refs. 9 & 18] and noting that

\[jh = jk a_1 \quad \text{[ref. 18; eq. (4)]} \quad \Gamma = \gamma_1 = \alpha + j\beta \quad \text{[ref. 9; eq. (1)]}\]

then eq. 2.2.14 above, as shown by Wassilieff [ref. 9], reveals the factor of \(\rho c^2 / K\) missing from eq. (12) Kurze and Vér [ref. 18].

Further, comparison of the above analysis with that of [ref. 9] shows that \(\rho / \rho'\) in [ref. 5] can be written as \(\rho \cdot c k / Z_a h\) which on writing

\[\Gamma_a = jh = \alpha + j\beta;\]

gives

\[M = -j\rho \cdot c k / Z_a \Gamma_a \quad\quad (2.2.15)\]

which can be identified with line 9230 in the algorithm 'Extended'. Here the value of \(P_0/P'_0\) is obtained directly from the values of \(\Gamma_a\) and \(Z_a\) derived from the Delany and Bazley formulae eqs. 2.1.1 and 2.2.2 as written into the subroutine 'Material'.
